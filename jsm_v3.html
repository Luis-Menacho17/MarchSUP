What the team celebrated
Strengths and bright spots acknowledged during the September review
Student goal-setting: Kudos given on student goal conferences — the idea that students should know their reading/math levels and have a personal roadmap to success was explicitly praised.
Academic growth: A 9% jump in math and ELA and a 28% jump in science were highlighted as strong progress.

Questions raised & areas the team pushed on
What the supportive colleagues and critical friends raised during the session
Tracking community events: "Do you take surveys from school events (fun days) to see if they help? How do you track?" — The answer was that Student Council surveys students and brings results to the SPMT.
Adult actions behind academic gains: "Has the team unpacked the adult actions that helped with the increase?" — A direct ask to connect adult practice to student outcomes.
Family engagement & immigration climate: "What does family engagement look like, specifically with the current social climate around immigration?" — Team was asked to think collectively about how to reach families with current fears, and how to leverage Apptegy for that.
Reluctant families: "What plan do you have to get reluctant parents/families into the school?" — Response: Frontload with academic activities (literacy/math coach in-service at home) before fun family events, rather than fun first.
Barriers to attendance: "What barriers remain for children who don't want to come to school?" — Identified as school culture & climate. Noted that CA was ~10% pre-pandemic and rose to 27% after.
Accountability vs empathy in attendance: "How do you balance accountability vs empathy?" — Response: Wrap-around services, making school inviting, reopening the pool as an incentive, individual goal-setting with students.

🎯 Action plan — Literacy
Our literacy strategy is grade-differentiated by skill level, grounded in play-based and place-based learning across all grades.
K–1
Daily phonological awareness small groups grounded in play-based and place-based language experiences — storytelling walks, environmental print, and outdoor literacy exploration.
Gr 2–3
Decoding intervention 2×/week, deliberately connected to hands-on science vocabulary so language learning is anchored in real context.
Gr 4–8
Evidence writing embedded across all content areas using RACES/RACECER frames within project-based units — moving academic argumentation from a literacy skill into a school-wide practice.
ML Gr 4–8
41–69% of ML students in Grades 4–6 remain in the lowest NWEA reading band. Our added TESOL teacher and paraeducator are focused here. We are advocating for a district-funded bridge-to-English model to match the structured support that exists in K–3 through Lectura.

SPMT & SSST — whole-school support frameworks
Two coordinated structures that ensure no student or staff member falls through the cracks
👥 SPMT — School Planning & Management Team
The SPMT serves as our school’s strategic planning body — bringing together school leadership, staff, families, and community partners to set direction, review data, and ensure our plans are aligned to student and school needs. It is the engine of our continuous improvement cycle.
🩹 SSST — School Supportive Services Team
The SSST coordinates wraparound support for students with academic, behavioral, or social-emotional needs. The team — including the counselor, social worker, and administrators — meets regularly to review referrals, develop intervention plans, and ensure every student has access to the support they need to be present and ready to learn.
🎯 Action plan — Climate & culture
Culture is built in the details. Our approach layers school-wide structures with individual support systems so every student and adult is held.
Daily entry
Every staff member greets every student by name at entry, every day. Belonging starts before the first bell.
Second Step
Implemented school-wide — structured SEL embedded in the daily schedule: self-regulation, empathy, and community built as skills, not added on as extras.
SPMT
School Planning & Management Team — sets strategic direction through data and ensures our plans align to student and school needs. Student Council results feed directly into SPMT.
SSST
School Supportive Services Team — coordinates wraparound support for individual students through the counselor, social worker, and administrators. No student falls through the cracks.
Place-based
Outdoor time, board games at lunch, and peer mediation are intentional belonging structures. Expanded peer mediation and grade-level town halls continue through spring.
Staff–student rel.
Held flat at 60% on the climate survey — our one area of focused attention. Targeted advisory structures and mentorship pairing are the spring response.

👁 We notice...
Data-grounded observations from this year
Math fact fluency grew significantly across all grade levels — Grade 3 alone jumped from 5% to 55% proficient or goal.
iReady: 72% of students making typical annual math growth — 3rd in the district. Students 3+ grades behind dropped from 34% to 20%.
Spanish literacy (Lectura): K–3 well-below dropped 52% → 35%. Kindergarten specifically: 65% → 25% well-below — a 40-point swing.
Students who "want to come to school every day" rose from 34% to 47%. Zero suspensions this year (down from 1).
53–58% of Grades 3–4 students remain in the lowest NWEA reading band, despite above-average growth rates.
ML students in Grades 3–6: 41–69% in the lowest reading band. Support structures weaken after Grade 3.
Chronic absenteeism at 23.3% — above the 20.9% EOY goal. Need ~23 more students to exit chronic absence by June.
Family climate survey response: only 9%. Class Dojo → Apptegy transition created friction that has not yet been resolved.
💭 We wonder...
Questions this data raises for the team
What specific adult practices drove the fluency growth — and how do we document and replicate them across grades?

⚠ Impeding factors
What is getting in the way of full implementation and progress
Class size above district average
JSM class sizes are above the district average, which creates challenges for differentiated instruction — particularly given that 51% of students are multilingual learners and 14.4% have IEPs. Larger classes make it harder for teachers to provide the individualized attention our students need.
Capacity
Title I tutoring budget limits our ability to act on targeting data — we know which students need it, but don't have the staffing to deliver it at scale.
Staffing
FMLA absences and inconsistent substitute coverage disrupt instructional continuity — particularly in grades with high ML or IEP concentrations.
ML Equity
JSM has added an additional TESOL teacher and paraeducator — a meaningful investment in ML support. These additional supports are intentionally focused on Grades 4–8, where ML literacy structures currently fade. 41–69% of ML students in Grades 4–6 remain in the lowest NWEA reading band, and the upper grades lack the structured supports that exist in K–3 through Lectura. We are asking for a district-funded bridge-to-English model specifically for Grades 4–8 to match the investment we have made in the lower grades.
IEP Load & Enrollment
72 students with IEPs (14.4%) — and growing. Students are being enrolled at JSM without advance identification of support needs; many arrive mid-year with existing IEPs, expanding caseloads with no corresponding staffing increase. Grades 3–5 IEP students: 70–100% in the lowest NWEA reading band.
Budget
We have nothing left to give up at the school level. Every position, resource, and program is serving a direct student need. Any right-sizing effort that does not account for our population — 94% high needs, 51% multilingual learners, 72 students with IEPs — would immediately harm the students who need us most.
Families
Immigration-related fears are keeping families away from evening and afterschool events. Family climate survey response was 9% — below any meaningful threshold for data-informed decisions.
Technology
Apptegy onboarding is still incomplete. Many families have not fully transitioned from Class Dojo — communication gaps are a direct contributor to low family engagement metrics.

What might we still need?
Specific asks from central office — each backed by data
Urgent
Bridge-to-English literacy support for Grades 4-6
41-69% of our multilingual learners in Grades 4-6 are in the lowest NWEA reading band. K-3 has Lectura — no equivalent structure exists for upper grades. Asking for a district-funded model for ML literacy in Grades 4-6.
Urgent
Co-teaching resources for students with IEPs in Grades 3-5
70-100% of IEP students in Grades 3-5 are in the lowest NWEA reading band. With 72 students with IEPs (14.4%), this is our largest single subgroup demand. Asking for additional co-teaching FTE or SPED reading specialists.
✓ Completed
DataWise fidelity visit from a district supervisor
A district supervisor completed a fidelity visit this year — a meaningful support for our first year of school-wide implementation. The visit strengthens our foundation heading into year two.
Ongoing
Structured cross-school collaboration time
Time with peer principals — even one structured learning walk per semester — would meaningfully accelerate our instructional growth. Requested in September and still needed.
Ongoing
More user-friendly family and student survey tools
The Custom Insights climate survey platform is not user-friendly for students or families. A 9% family response rate reflects a platform accessibility problem. Asking for district consideration of a more accessible survey tool for the next cycle.
